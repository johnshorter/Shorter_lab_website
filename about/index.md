## My path

I didn't start out studying people. My way into genetics began with insects — honeybees and paper wasps during my undergraduate and master's years, and then fruit flies during my PhD at North Carolina State University, where I worked on the genetics of behavior. As a postdoc at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, I moved to mouse genetics and the Collaborative Cross, learning how to map complex traits across whole genomes.

In 2019 I moved to Denmark, drawn by its extraordinary national health registries and biobanks — some of the best resources in the world for studying human disease at scale. Today my work draws on the Danish Blood Donor Study, iPSYCH, and the Copenhagen Hospital Biobank, alongside resources like the UK Biobank, to study the genetic architecture of psychiatric and other complex diseases — and increasingly to ask how a person's genes shape their response to treatment.

What ties it all together is a fascination with how genetic variation makes us who we are, and a conviction that careful data science can help medicine fit the individual rather than the average. Alongside the research, I love teaching and mentoring the next generation of scientists in genetics, bioinformatics, and translational medicine.
