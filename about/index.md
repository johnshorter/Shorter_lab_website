## My path

I didn't start out studying people. My way into genetics began with insects: honeybees and paper wasps during my undergraduate and master's years, then fruit flies during my PhD at North Carolina State University, where I studied the genetics of behavior. As a postdoc at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, I switched to mouse genetics and the Collaborative Cross, and learned how to map complex traits across whole genomes.

In 2019 I moved to Denmark, drawn by its national health registries and biobanks, which are among the best resources anywhere for studying human disease at scale. Today my work draws on the Danish Blood Donor Study, iPSYCH, and the Copenhagen Hospital Biobank, alongside the UK Biobank, to study the genetic architecture of psychiatric and other complex diseases. More and more, I want to know how a person's genes shape their response to treatment.

Underneath all of it is a fascination with how genetic variation makes us who we are, and a belief that careful data science can help medicine fit the individual instead of the average. I also care a great deal about teaching, and about mentoring the students and early-career researchers who come through the lab.
